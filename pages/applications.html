Alma 31 · Applications

Study applications

Personal study ideas, family activities, and teaching resources — transforming the lessons of Alma 31 into meaningful practice.

Personal study

1. Prayer self-assessment (15–20 min)

Compare your personal prayers to both the Zoramite prayer and Alma's prayer to identify areas for growth.

  1. Read Alma 31:15–18 (Zoramite prayer) and 31:26–35 (Alma's prayer).
  2. Write down characteristics of each prayer.
  3. Reflect on your recent prayers — which do they resemble more?
  4. Identify two or three specific improvements.
  5. Practice a new style of prayer this week incorporating those insights.

2. Pride inventory (30–45 min)

Honest self-examination to identify where pride may be affecting your spiritual growth and relationships.

  1. List the ways Zoramites showed pride (verses 24–28).
  2. For each, honestly assess your own tendencies.
  3. Identify your personal "Rameumptoms" — areas where you seek recognition.
  4. Write specific goals for developing humility.
  5. Plan concrete actions to implement these changes.

Pride indicators to consider: comparing yourself to others, difficulty accepting correction, seeking recognition for good deeds, judging others' spiritual worthiness, focusing on external religious performance.

3. Word power study (25–35 min)

Deep dive into Alma 31:5 and the power of God's word to transform hearts and minds.

  1. Study Alma 31:5 in depth.
  2. Look up cross-references about God's word: Hebrews 4:12, Isaiah 55:11, Alma 32:28.
  3. Journal about times God's word has changed your heart.
  4. Identify someone who needs the power of God's word.
  5. Plan how you can share appropriate scriptures with them.

4. Compassion challenge (ongoing)

Follow Alma's example by developing Christ-like compassion for those who have lost their way spiritually.

  1. Identify someone struggling with faith or making poor choices.
  2. Study Alma's response to the Zoramites (verses 24–35).
  3. Pray for that person daily using Alma's prayer as a model.
  4. Look for opportunities to show love without judgment.
  5. Journal about insights gained through this practice.

Family activities

For young children (ages 3–8)

  • Build a Rameumptom and contrast. Use blocks to build a tall, narrow tower (Rameumptom) and a wide, low circle of chairs (family prayer). Talk about the difference between standing high alone vs. kneeling together.
  • Drawing activity. Have children draw a picture of "kind prayer" vs. "showing-off prayer." Talk about the feelings each picture gives them.
  • Caring hearts. Make paper hearts with names of friends, family, or neighbors who need extra love. Use them as prompts during family prayer.

For older children (ages 9–12)

  • Compare-and-contrast game. Make a T-chart: "Zoramite prayer" vs. "Alma's prayer." Have children fill in differences. Discuss what each says about the person praying.
  • Modern Rameumptoms. Discuss what modern "Rameumptoms" might look like — places people go to be seen rather than to draw closer to God.
  • Word power experiment. Read a favorite gospel verse together. Pick one phrase. Talk for ten minutes about what it means to each family member.

For teens (ages 13–18)

  • Pride and social media. Discuss how social media can become a Rameumptom — a place to be seen rather than to grow. What does authentic faith look like online?
  • Compassion mapping. Identify one person in your life who feels excluded. Brainstorm specific, age-appropriate ways to include and minister to them this week.
  • Personal prayer journal. Start a private prayer journal modeled after Alma's prayer: honest emotion, intercession for others, requests for strength.

Teaching ideas

For Primary teachers

  • Use simple visuals (tall tower vs. family circle) to teach the contrast between prideful and humble prayer.
  • Tell the story in age-appropriate language — emphasize that God hears all sincere prayers, no matter how simple.
  • End with a class prayer where children take turns adding one specific thing they're grateful for or one person they want to pray for.

For Sunday School / Seminary

  • Open with the central question: "What's the difference between performing faith and living faith?"
  • Read verses 15–18 (Zoramite prayer) and 26–35 (Alma's prayer) side by side. Have students identify five contrasts between them.
  • Discuss verse 5 and the principle that the word of God is more powerful than the sword. Where do students see this principle proven (or not) in their own lives?
  • Close with a personal-application moment: "What would Alma pray about if he were standing where you stand today?"

For Relief Society / Elders Quorum

  • Focus the discussion on Alma's compassion (verses 24–35). How does Alma respond to people who have rejected what we believe?
  • Explore "Behold, their souls are precious" (verse 35) as a framework for ministering to those who have stepped away from the faith.
  • Share experiences (anonymously if needed) of times people have shown Christ-like compassion in difficult situations.

Discussion questions

  1. What motivates my prayers — genuine need, habit, or appearance?
  2. How often do I pray for others versus myself?
  3. Why did Alma choose the word over force? Where in my life could I do the same?
  4. What does the Rameumptom look like today?
  5. How do I show compassion for the spiritually afflicted without being condescending?
  6. How is Alma's prayer different from prayers I've heard in church? From prayers I've offered?
  7. What does verse 5 — "the virtue of the word" — mean in a moment when I feel powerless to change someone?

Self-reflection prompts

Use these for journaling, family discussion, or quiet reflection:

  • Am I building Rameumptoms in my spiritual life — seeking elevation above others?
  • When I encounter people who have lost faith, is my first response judgment or compassion?
  • How does my faith influence my daily interactions, decisions, and priorities?
  • Do I truly believe in the power of God's word to change hearts, including my own?
  • What is one specific thing I can do this week to make my private worship more authentic?
