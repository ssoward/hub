Easter Week · Day 8

Easter Sunday

He Is Risen!
An Easter Week Art Display · Brad Young
Day 8 — the greatest day in the history of the world. The tomb is empty. Death is conquered. The Resurrection is real. Everything the Savior promised, everything He suffered to accomplish — it is finished and it is triumphant. He is risen. Press S to open speaker notes.
The stone is rolled away. The tomb is empty. The linen cloths lie where the body had been. Mary Magdalene and the other women came at dawn, bringing spices to anoint a dead body — and found nothing but an empty room and two angels.
"And they found the stone rolled away from the sepulchre. And they entered in, and found not the body of the Lord Jesus.

"And it came to pass, as they were much perplexed thereabout, behold, two men stood by them in shining garments:

"And as they were afraid, and bowed down their faces to the earth, they said unto them, Why seek ye the living among the dead? He is not here, but is risen."
— Luke 24:2–6
"Why seek ye the living among the dead?" The angel's question echoes through the ages. We sometimes look for Christ in the cold and the dead — in past experience, in ritual without power. He is not there. He is risen. He is living. He is present.
Matthew records that the angel's appearance was "like lightning, and his raiment white as snow: and for fear of him the keepers did shake, and became as dead men." (Matthew 28:3-4) Heaven announced the Resurrection with power.
"And, behold, there was a great earthquake: for the angel of the Lord descended from heaven, and came and rolled back the stone from the door, and sat upon it. His countenance was like lightning, and his raiment white as snow…

"And the angel answered and said unto the women, Fear not ye: for I know that ye seek Jesus, which was crucified. He is not here: for he is risen, as he said. Come, see the place where the Lord lay."
— Matthew 28:2–3, 5–6
"As he said." The angel pointed back to Christ's own words. He had promised this. The Resurrection was not a surprise to heaven — it was the fulfillment of a covenant. "Come, see the place where the Lord lay." Not where He lies. Where He lay. Past tense. He is gone from the tomb forever.
Mary Magdalene, weeping outside the tomb, turns and sees someone she thinks is the gardener. Then He speaks her name: "Mary." One word. And everything changes. She was the first to see the risen Lord — the woman out of whom He had cast seven devils. Grace personified.
"But Mary stood without at the sepulchre weeping: and as she wept, she stooped down, and looked into the sepulchre…

"And when she had thus said, she turned herself back, and saw Jesus standing, and knew not that it was Jesus…

"Jesus saith unto her, Mary. She turned herself, and saith unto him, Rabboni; which is to say, Master."
— John 20:11, 14–16
The most tender moment of the Resurrection account — her name, spoken by the one she thought was dead. The Good Shepherd calls His own sheep by name. He calls us each by name. Not "woman." Not "you." Mary. And she knew Him.
That evening, Jesus appeared to the Apostles gathered behind locked doors. "Peace be unto you," He said — and showed them His hands and His side. Thomas was not present and doubted. A week later, Jesus returned specifically for Thomas. "Reach hither thy finger, and behold my hands."
"And as they thus spake, Jesus himself stood in the midst of them, and saith unto them, Peace be unto you.

"But they were terrified and affrighted, and supposed that they had seen a spirit. And he said unto them, Why are ye troubled? and why do thoughts arise in your hearts?

"Behold my hands and my feet, that it is I myself: handle me, and see; for a spirit hath not flesh and bones, as ye see me have."
— Luke 24:36–39
"A spirit hath not flesh and bones, as ye see me have." The Resurrection is physical, literal, and eternal. Jesus did not become a ghost or a spiritual impression. He stands before them as a glorified, resurrected Being — the firstfruits of them that slept. (1 Corinthians 15:20)
The risen, glorified, triumphant Savior. Death is swallowed up in victory. The grave has no lasting claim. Because He lives, we shall live. Because He rose, we will rise. The Easter promise is not wishful thinking — it is the most thoroughly attested event in human history.
The glory of what was accomplished. A perfect, infinite Atonement — complete. A Resurrection that opens the door for all mankind — complete. A covenant that reaches from before the foundation of the world to eternity — sealed. He is risen. He lives. And because He does, so shall we.
He Is Risen
as he said
"I am the resurrection, and the life: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live…

For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life."
— John 11:25 · John 3:16
Two verses that together encompass the whole of the gospel: the power of the Resurrection and the motive of love. He rose because He could — infinite and eternal. He did it because He loved — infinite and eternal. This is Easter. This is the good news.
