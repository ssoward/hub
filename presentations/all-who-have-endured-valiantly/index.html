General Conference · Doctrine & Covenants 121:29

All Who Have
Endured Valiantly

"All thrones and dominions, principalities and powers, shall be revealed and set forth
upon all who have endured valiantly for the gospel of Jesus Christ."
Elder David A. Bednar
Of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles
Welcome. Today we study Elder David A. Bednar's powerful conference address, "All Who Have Endured Valiantly." The title comes from Doctrine and Covenants 121:29 — a promise from the Lord to those who endure valiantly for the gospel. Elder Bednar opens with a personal reflection on running and endurance, then elevates that concept far above mere physical persistence into something joyous and eternal. Two Kinds of Enduring

Physical

"Maintaining intense physical and mental effort over an extended period of time"

Sore muscles. Aching lungs. Exhaustion. Grit your teeth and push through.

Spiritual

"Far more than merely persevering tenaciously to complete demanding duties or challenges"

A joyous quest. A pressing forward with faith in Jesus Christ.

"Look unto me, and endure to the end, and ye shall live; for unto him that endureth to the end will I give eternal life."
— 3 Nephi 15:9
Elder Bednar opens with his love-hate relationship with running — he loves the benefits and exhilaration, but hates the sore muscles, aching lungs, and exhaustion. He uses that physical metaphor to draw a critical distinction. Physical endurance is about grit, persistence, willpower — pushing through pain. Spiritual endurance is categorically different. It involves something far more transformative than white-knuckling to the finish. The Lord's promise is eternal life — not just survival. That should tell us something about the nature of spiritual enduring.

Becoming New Creatures in Christ

"The Savior's restored gospel invites us to be spiritually transformed — not merely to improve our behavior."
Come Unto Him

Draw near through covenant, ordinance, and sincere discipleship

Be Born Again

Experience a mighty change of heart — not improved habits, but a new nature

Receive Fullness

Receive a fulness of the Holy Ghost through merits, mercy, and grace

Put Off Natural Man

Through the Atonement, overcome the self-centered, inward-turning nature

Become Saints

Consecrate heart, might, mind and strength in the Lord's service

Absolute Need

Possible only "through the merits, mercy, and grace of the Holy Messiah"

Elder Bednar makes a crucial distinction: the gospel doesn't just ask us to behave better. It invites us to BE transformed. This is a comprehensive change — in character, desires, actions, and what we truly love. As we align these more closely with God's will, the Savior can bring about a complete change in us. The natural man turns inward. The new creature in Christ turns outward in love and service — even in adversity. This transformation is not a product of our own willpower. It is "possible only through the merits, mercy, and grace of the Holy Messiah." We are in absolute and never-ending need of the Savior's help.

The Role of Spiritual Gifts

Blessings and capacities given by God through the power of the Holy Ghost

All who receive the gift of the Holy Ghost are eligible to receive spiritual gifts — intended "to bless and benefit those who love the Lord and seek to keep His commandments."

Prerequisites — Not Rewards

Spiritual gifts are prerequisites for coming unto the Savior — not trophies earned through personal discipline and focused effort alone

Given, Not Earned

"All such gifts are given by God according to His will and timing; they are not results we earn solely through focused effort and personal discipline"

Why does Elder Bednar introduce spiritual gifts here? Because it sets up the key insight about charity. Spiritual gifts — including charity — are not achievements we unlock through our own willpower. They are gifts from God, given according to HIS will and timing. This reframes the entire discussion of "enduring to the end." We are not trying to grind our way to salvation through sheer willpower. We are receiving gifts. We are being changed by God. The very name "spiritual gifts" teaches a vital lesson. The agency and effort are ours — but the transforming power comes from God.

Charity

The Pure Love of Christ · The Greatest Spiritual Gift
"Mormon testified that 'charity is the pure love of Christ' and 'the greatest of all' spiritual gifts. Significantly, the word endure is used in the scriptures to define and describe charity."
"Endureth Forever"

Moroni 7:46–47

"Beareth All Things"

1 Corinthians 13:7

"Never Faileth"

1 Corinthians 13:8

Now comes the central insight of the talk. The word "endure" is used in scripture to DEFINE charity. "Charity endureth forever. Suffereth long. Beareth all things. Endureth all things." This is not coincidence. The connection between charity and enduring is deep and intentional. "Charity never faileth" — sisters in the audience would recognize this well. The question Elder Bednar is building toward: what if charity IS the "end" we are enduring toward?

Possessed of Charity

"Whoso is found possessed of [the spiritual gift of charity] at the last day, it shall be well with him." — Mormon
We Can Possess Charity

We work toward it. We seek it. We pray for it. We exercise acts of love, compassion, and service.

"Give, and it shall be given unto you"

Charity Can Possess Us

Through God's grace, charity becomes our nature. We stop performing acts of love — we become loving beings.

"That ye may be filled with this love"

"Note the double meaning of the word of in this verse. We can possess charity, but ultimately charity should possess us."
— Elder Bednar
This is one of Elder Bednar's most elegant insights. The double meaning of the word "of." Being "possessed OF" charity — this describes acts of love and compassion we perform. It's active. We can choose to do loving things. But being "POSSESSED" by charity — this describes a transformation of our very nature. Charity becomes us. We love as the Savior loves because we have been spiritually changed at a fundamental level. The goal is not just to DO more loving things. The goal is to BECOME a loving person — a new creature in Christ whose default orientation is outward, toward others.

Higher and Holier

Two dimensions of charity — as seen in the 13th Article of Faith
First Half — What We Do

"We believe in being honest, true, chaste, benevolent, virtuous, and in doing good to all men."

Charitable and Gracious Acts
Second Half — What We Become

"We believe all things, we hope all things, we have endured many things, and hope to be able to endure all things."

Ongoing Spiritual Transformation
"An admonition for us to do good, to be good, and to endure to the end in coming unto and following the Savior
and receiving the heavenly gift to love as He loves."
Elder Bednar sees the 13th Article of Faith as a perfect summary of the two dimensions of charity. First dimension: Acts of charity. Doing good. Honesty, benevolence, virtue. These are the behaviors of a loving person. Second dimension: Becoming. The ongoing transformation. "We have endured many things, and hope to endure all things." This is not just behavior — it's the direction of the entire soul. The conclusion of the 13th Article — "if there is anything virtuous, lovely, or of good report or praiseworthy, we seek after these things" — describes someone who is possessed of charity. They naturally seek what is good, because goodness has become their nature.

The Savior Turned Outward

Natural Man — Turns Inward
  • — Self-interest
  • — Self-centeredness
  • — Selfishness
  • Turns inward in adversity and pain
The Savior — Turned Outward
  • + Compassion for others
  • + Service in adversity
  • + Love under pain
  • Turned outward even in spiritual adversity and physical pain
"We do not merely perform benevolent deeds; rather, our state of being is changed and becomes increasingly Christlike. Charity, then, ultimately possesses us."
— Elder Bednar
This is the heart of the talk. The Savior's divine nature and sublime character were the wellsprings of perfect compassion during His mortal ministry. When He faced spiritual adversity or physical pain, He turned OUTWARD in love and service. The natural man — the unredeemed part of each of us — turns INWARD: self-interest, self-centeredness, selfishness. As we become new creatures in Christ, this pattern of turning outward becomes more natural. It's not just behavior. Our state of being changes. We notice others' needs. We serve instinctively. Charity possesses us.

Grace for Grace

The Pattern of Spiritual Development

"He received not of the fulness at first, but [received and] continued … grace to grace, until he received a fulness."

— Doctrine & Covenants 93:13

1 · We Were There

Jesus Christ "was in the beginning with the Father." So were we — this is our eternal origin

2 · He Showed the Way

"He marked the path and led the way" — progressing grace to grace throughout His mortal life

3 · We Follow

We can receive of His fulness "in due time" — following the same "grace for grace" pattern

"The prospect of being blessed in mortality with even a small portion of the character and attributes of Jesus may seem utterly impossible to us. But the Redeemer 'marked the path and led the way.'"
Elder Bednar now makes the eternal case that this transformation is achievable — not in one leap, but grace upon grace. The Savior Himself "received not of the fulness at first." He progressed grace to grace until He received a fulness. This is the pattern for us as well. We were with the Father in the beginning. We came here. We follow the same pattern of progression the Savior modeled. The transformation to become possessed of charity — to become new creatures in Christ — doesn't happen overnight. It's a lifetime process. A joyous quest. Grace for grace. This is why "enduring to the end" is a lifetime journey, not a marathon finish line.

The Key Substitution

Replace "endureth to the end" with "is possessed of the pure love of Christ"

"He that endureth to the end [or is possessed of the pure love of Christ] shall be saved."

— The Savior, to His ancient Apostles · Matthew 10:22

"He that endureth to the end [or is possessed of the pure love of Christ], the same shall be saved."

— Nephi · 2 Nephi 31:20

"He that findeth mercy and endureth to the end [or is possessed of the pure love of Christ] the same shall be saved."

— Alma · Alma 32:43
"Enduring to the end is linked inextricably to the spiritual gift of charity." — Elder Bednar
This is the powerful teaching moment of Elder Bednar's talk. He takes three familiar scriptures about enduring to the end and inserts the phrase "is possessed of the pure love of Christ." In each case, the substitution fits perfectly. Because charity IS the end toward which we are enduring. To endure to the end IS to be possessed by the pure love of Christ. This isn't just a word swap. It's revealing something profound that was always there in the doctrine — the destination of enduring is BECOMING charitable. Becoming Christlike. Being possessed of His love. Let these three witnesses sink in. Three prophets — the Savior, Nephi, and Alma — all pointing to the same truth.

The Joyous Quest

"Enduring to the end is the joyous quest of a lifetime — a pressing forward with faith in Jesus Christ in a gradual process of trusting in and receiving help from our Savior to become more like Him. As our love for Him grows ever stronger and deeper, we can be blessed to receive spiritual perspective, the Lord's empowering grace, and exceedingly great and indescribable joy."
Spiritual Perspective

See with eternal eyes, not temporal fear

Empowering Grace

The Lord's enabling power sustains us daily

Indescribable Joy

Not only at the end — but now, along the way

Here is the emotional and spiritual heart of the talk. Elder Bednar reframes enduring completely. It is NOT grim determination. It is NOT sore muscles and aching lungs. It is NOT white-knuckling our way to the finish. Enduring to the end is a JOYOUS QUEST. A pressing forward with faith. A gradual process of trusting in and receiving help from the Savior. The rewards — perspective, grace, joy — don't wait until the end. They come along the way, as we deepen our love for the Savior. "Exceedingly great and indescribable joy." That is the life of a disciple who is becoming possessed of charity.
