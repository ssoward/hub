Last Supper & Gethsemane
An Easter Week Art Display · Brad Young
This is Day 5 of the Easter Week Art Display series by Brad Young. Thursday covers two of the most sacred events of the Savior's final hours: the Last Supper in the upper room and the agony in the Garden of Gethsemane. Press S to open speaker notes. Use arrow keys or click to advance slides.
This painting depicts the Last Supper — Jesus Christ reclining with His Twelve Apostles on the night before His crucifixion. The warm candlelit tones capture the intimacy and gravity of this Passover meal that would become the first sacrament.
"Jesus took bread, and blessed it, and brake it, and gave it to the disciples, and said, Take, eat; this is my body. And he took the cup, and gave thanks, and gave it to them, saying, Drink ye all of it; For this is my blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins…I will not drink henceforth of this fruit of the vine, until that day when I drink it new with you in my Father's kingdom."
— Matthew 26:26–29
In this moment Jesus institutes the sacrament — the ordinance by which we remember His body and blood. He looks forward to drinking it "new" in His Father's kingdom, a promise of the Resurrection and the joyful reunion yet to come.
During the same upper room gathering, Jesus rose from the meal, wrapped himself with a towel, and began washing the feet of His disciples — a task reserved for the lowest household servants. This act of profound humility stunned the Apostles.
"He riseth from supper, and laid aside his garments; and took a towel, and girded himself. After that he poureth water into a basin, and began to wash the disciples' feet, and to wipe them with the towel wherewith he was girded.

"What I do thou knowest not now; but thou shalt know hereafter… If I wash thee not, thou hast no part with me."
— John 13:4–5, 7–8
Peter initially refused, not understanding. But Christ's answer was decisive: to receive Him fully, we must allow ourselves to be served and cleansed by Him. This is a model of covenant relationship — His grace washing away what we cannot clean ourselves.
An ancient olive press — a heavy stone wheel crushing olives to extract every drop of oil. This is the physical image behind the name "Gethsemane." Just as olives are pressed to yield their essence, the Savior would be pressed to yield His infinite atoning sacrifice.
Gethsemane
Hebrew: Gat Shmanim · "olive press"
"Then cometh Jesus with them unto a place called Gethsemane [meaning olive press, an image of the weight He would soon bear as part of His Infinite and Eternal Atonement]."
— Matthew 26:36
The very name of the garden is a prophecy. Gethsemane — the olive press — foreshadows the infinite weight that would be placed upon the Savior. Just as oil cannot be extracted without crushing, the Atonement could not be completed without the full weight of all human suffering pressing upon the Son of God.
This painting captures the agony of Gethsemane — the Savior prostrate, bearing the weight of all sin, all suffering, all pain ever experienced. A single shaft of light penetrates the darkness: the presence of the Father, even in the deepest moment of anguish.
"And [He] began to be sorrowful and very heavy… My soul is exceeding sorrowful, even unto death.

"…He said, Abba, Father, all things are possible unto thee; take away this cup from me: nevertheless not what I will, but what thou wilt…

"And being in an agony he prayed more earnestly: and his sweat was as it were great drops of blood falling down to the ground."
— Matthew 26:36–38 · Mark 14:36 · Luke 22:44
Three gospel accounts converge on this moment of infinite suffering. "Not what I will, but what thou wilt" — the perfect submission of the Son to the Father, even in extremity. This is the heart of the Atonement: the Savior chose, in full knowledge and anguish, to drink the cup for each of us. We are known. We are loved. We are redeemed.
