Welcome. Today we study Sister Camille N. Johnson's address from the 2026 BYU Women's Conference, "Throwing the Door Wide Open to More Jesus Christ in Our Lives." The image comes from Revelation 3:20 — Christ standing at the door and knocking. Her invitation is not to add more to our lives, but to open the door wider and let Him in more fully. When Hard Times Become Holy

"When you do hard things with Jesus Christ,
the hard times become holy."

What

Life includes struggles we cannot eliminate.

Why

Difficulty becomes sanctifying when Christ is in it.

How

Specific practices deepen our relationship with Him.

Her central thesis: hard seasons become holy seasons when Jesus Christ is present. Faith may not remove the hardship — but it can transform it. She organizes the talk around three questions. What: struggle is inherent to mortality. Why: difficulty is sanctifying when the Savior is involved. How: concrete, deepened practices bring Him closer. The rest of the talk is the "how."

Women of Light

Johnson opens by seeing before her "women of light" — disciples of Jesus Christ. She recalls Eliza R. Snow's image of coals of fire that burn brighter when gathered together. Community is not incidental to discipleship; it strengthens it.
"Arise, shine; for thy light is come, and the glory of the Lord is risen upon thee."
— Isaiah 60:1 · see also 3 Nephi 18:24
She frames her audience as women of light and disciples, invoking Eliza R. Snow's metaphor: individual coals cool quickly alone, but gathered together they burn hot and bright. The gospel is meant to be lived in community — sisters strengthen one another's fire.
Revelation 3:20

Throw the Door Wide Open

"Behold, I stand at the door, and knock: if any man hear my voice, and open the door, I will come in to him."
— Revelation 3:20

Less

Reduce time given to lesser priorities.

More

Deepen the spiritual practices you already keep.

The organizing image: Christ is already at the door, knocking. He waits on our agency to open it. To "throw the door wide open" has two movements — subtract lesser things, and go deeper with the practices we already have. Not more items on the list; more of Him.

More Jesus, Less of Lesser Things

President Dallin H. Oaks teaches that desires shape our destiny:
Desire→ Priorities→ Choices→ Actions
The honest question: "Do you desire more Jesus Christ in your life?" She names scrolling as a common form of emotional avoidance — and invites us to notice the trigger (boredom, loneliness, anger, stress) and turn to the Savior instead.
Oaks: our desires determine our trajectory. Desire shapes priorities, priorities shape choices, choices become actions. So the first question isn't "what should I do?" but "what do I actually want?" Her concrete example is the phone. Scrolling is often emotional avoidance. The invitation: catch the trigger — boredom, loneliness, anger, stress — and, in that moment, seek the Savior rather than the screen. That's "less of lesser things" made practical.

Mary & Martha: Good vs. Best

The moment

Martha served with good intentions but missed the "good part" — sitting at the feet of Jesus, as Mary did.

Later

At Lazarus's tomb, Martha declares unwavering testimony: "I believe that thou art the Christ."

Good works matter — but direct connection with Christ is the "best part."
— Luke 10:38–42 · John 11:21–27
Two scenes with Martha. First, the familiar one: she is "cumbered about much serving" while Mary sits with Jesus. He gently tells her Mary has chosen the good part. The point is not that service is bad — it is that presence with Christ is better. Second, at Lazarus's tomb, Martha delivers one of scripture's great testimonies. The woman who was once distracted becomes unshakable. Deepened connection with Christ over time produces that kind of faith.

Not a New Formula

"I am not inviting you to do something more — I am asking you to do more with what you are already doing."
Six familiar practices, deepened  ·  Prayer  ·  Scripture  ·  Sacrament  ·  Temple  ·  Forgiveness  ·  Service
Before the practical section, she disarms the fear of "one more thing." There's no new program here — just prophetic counsel to go deeper in six practices we already know. The next slides take each in turn.
Practice 1

Prayer

President Holland, after a near-death experience: "Pray more than you pray" — in more places, at more times through the day.
  • Pray at alert times — not only when drowsy at dawn and dusk
  • Be honest — talk with God about your actual concerns
  • Give specific gratitude, not general thanks
  • Treat prayer as a living discussion, not a recitation
Elder Holland's counsel after nearly dying: pray more than you pray. Not longer necessarily — more frequently, more places, more moments. Johnson's own evolution is the model. She used to pray only when tired. Now she prays when alert, more honestly about real concerns, with specific gratitude, treating it as an ongoing conversation with a Father who is listening — a living discussion rather than a script.
Practice 2

Scripture Study

Peru: Unable to speak Spanish, she read the Book of Mormon three times a year in Spanish. She never gained the gift of tongues — but gained the ability to love her missionaries in their language, and a deeper relationship with Christ.

Read → Study

Seek patterns, symbols, and types of Christ.

Walk with prophets

Study living prophets' words — a daily devotional with President Oaks, "Christ's mouthpiece today."

The Peru story: as a young missionary she couldn't speak Spanish, so she read the Book of Mormon in Spanish three times a year. The gift she received wasn't tongues — it was the capacity to communicate love in their language, and a strengthened personal bond with Christ through study in difficulty. The deepening: move from reading to studying — hunting for patterns, symbols, and types of Christ. And extend scripture study to the living prophets. After President Nelson's passing she began a daily devotional walking through President Oaks's counsel, testifying he is the Lord's mouthpiece today. Walking with prophetic counsel is walking with Jesus.
Practice 3

Sacrament Worship

Elder Patrick Kearon distinguishes attending (being present) from worshiping (intentionally praising in transformative ways).
  • Create a Sabbath atmosphere that invites self-reflection
  • Repent joyfully before partaking
  • During the blessing, focus on Gethsemane and the cross
Kearon's reframe: rather than tallying the week's mistakes, ponder how the Lord "relentlessly pursued you with wonderful love." Discover "the joy of daily repentance."
Kearon's distinction is the heart of this slide: you can attend without worshiping. Worship is intentional, transformative praise. Practical preparation: build a Sabbath atmosphere, come already repenting with joy, and during the sacrament focus on the Atonement — His suffering in Gethsemane and on the cross. The reframe is beautiful: instead of using the sacrament only to review your failures, use it to notice how relentlessly the Lord pursued you with love that week. Repentance becomes a daily joy, not a weekly audit.
Practice 4

Temple Worship

President Nelson: endowed women have "the power of God and angels having charge over them." Covenants are "sequential… additive, and synergistic."
  • Look purposefully for symbols and evidence of Christ in the ordinances
  • Receive the temple as a place of refuge and recalibration
  • Let the garment be a daily reflection on His life and sacrifice
Nelson's promise to endowed women is stunning — the power of God, and angels charged over them. Covenants aren't repetitive; they are sequential, additive, and synergistic — each one compounds the others. To deepen temple worship: go looking for Christ in every ordinance, treat the temple as refuge and recalibration from a noisy world, and let the garment be a daily, tactile reminder of His life and sacrifice. Everything taught in the temple increases understanding of Jesus Christ.
Practice 5

Repentance & Forgiveness

To refuse to forgive is to reject the Atonement. Forgiveness is not minimizing the harm — it is maximizing faith in Christ's power to compensate for it.
Elder Rasband: the Atonement is "very big compensation" for "very big harms." Christ's power covers both our sins and the sins of those who wound us.
— Matthew 6:14–15 · Doctrine and Covenants 64:9
This is the hardest practice. Johnson reframes forgiveness: it does not mean the harm was small. It means you trust the Atonement is big enough to answer it. Rasband: big harms require big compensation — and the Atonement is exactly that. The same power that covers our sins covers the sins committed against us. Refusing to forgive, per D&C 64:9, keeps us in the greater sin because it distrusts Christ's power. Forgiveness is an act of maximized faith.
Practice 6

Service — Relief Through Relief

Ministering with priesthood authority, women represent the Savior. To serve is to work with Christ to bless others — and, in the process, to find our own relief.
"For how knoweth a man the master whom he has not served…?"
— Mosiah 5:13
Service is partnership. When women minister with priesthood authority they represent the Savior — they are His hands. The Relief Society's mission is relief: temporal and spiritual. And the paradox is that in providing relief to others, we find our own. Mosiah 5:13: we come to know the Master by serving Him. Service isn't separate from knowing Christ — it's a primary way we know Him.
2 Nephi 33:6

I Glory in My Jesus

"I glory in plainness; I glory in truth; I glory in my Jesus, for he hath redeemed my soul."
— 2 Nephi 33:6
With Eliza R. Snow: "I will go forward… I will smile at the rage of the tempest." Jesus Christ is our relief, strength, song, and salvation — and He will sanctify affliction for our good.
She closes where she began: the invitation is not additive. Do more with what you already do — and the door opens wider to Christ. Eliza R. Snow's resolve — go forward, smile at the tempest, let the testimony of Jesus light the lamp — becomes the charge. Johnson's final testimony: Jesus Christ is relief, strength, song, and salvation. He may not remove the hardship, but He will make the hard holy. Bear testimony and close.
